Section 01
What Is USO?

The United States Oil Fund (USO) is an exchange-traded fund launched by United States Commodity Funds LLC (USCF). Its stated objective is to track the daily percentage changes in the spot price of West Texas Intermediate (WTI) light, sweet crude oil, as measured by the near-month futures contract on the New York Mercantile Exchange (NYMEX).

USO does not own physical oil barrels. It cannot — storing thousands of barrels of crude oil is impractical for a retail investment product. Instead, it holds futures contracts: legally binding agreements to buy a specific quantity of oil at a specific price on a specific future date. This distinction is the most important fact about USO and will be explored in depth in Section 3.

What USO IS
  • An exchange-traded fund (ETF) on the NYSE
  • A futures-based oil price proxy
  • A short-term trading vehicle for tactical oil exposure
  • Liquid and accessible — no commodity account needed
  • Useful during oil price spikes — especially in backwardation
What USO IS NOT
  • Not a direct investment in physical oil
  • Not a long-term buy-and-hold asset
  • Not an accurate tracker of spot oil prices over time
  • Not equivalent to owning oil company stocks
  • Not immune to structural return decay from futures rolling

Section 02
How USO Works

Futures Contracts, Not Spot Price

When you buy USO, you are not buying crude oil. You are buying shares in a fund that holds crude oil futures contracts. These contracts promise delivery of 1,000 barrels of oil at a specific date and location — Cushing, Oklahoma, the delivery hub for NYMEX-traded crude. Since the fund cannot take physical delivery, it must exit each contract before it expires and purchase the next month's contract. This is called rolling.

The Monthly Roll Cycle

Every month, without exception, USO executes this sequence:

  • USO holds the front-month WTI futures contract (e.g., April delivery)
  • Two weeks before expiration, USO begins selling the April contract
  • Simultaneously, it purchases the May delivery contract at the current market price
  • The price difference between these two contracts is the roll cost (or roll gain)
  • This cycle repeats every single month — 12 times per year, indefinitely

Contango vs. Backwardation

The price relationship between the expiring contract and the next one determines whether rolling is costly or profitable. This single variable — the shape of the futures curve — is the dominant driver of USO's long-run performance relative to oil prices.

Contango — The Drag
  • Future contracts priced higher than current spot
  • USO sells low, buys high every month
  • Generates negative roll yield — silent return erosion
  • The normal state of oil markets most of the time
  • Destroys long-term holders even when oil rises modestly
Backwardation — The Boost
  • Future contracts priced lower than current spot
  • USO sells high, buys low every month
  • Generates positive roll yield — amplifies gains
  • Occurs during supply shocks and geopolitical crises
  • Current condition as of March 2026 (Hormuz crisis)

The published roll schedule is public knowledge. Sophisticated traders — hedge funds, algorithmic desks — know exactly when USO must sell its expiring contracts and buy new ones. They can position ahead of these predictable moves, extracting value from USO's forced transactions at USO shareholders' expense. This is a structural, recurring cost that cannot be fully eliminated.

Section 03
The Contango Problem

"USO is a terrible place to be based on the contango. Being long the oil front-month and rolling into a contango market is not something you really want to be doing." — Oil analyst Stephen Schork, CNBC

The Math of Roll Decay

The numbers are stark and need to be internalized before any position decision. A 1% monthly contango cost translates to approximately 13% annualized drag — before the expense ratio. At 2% monthly, the drag reaches roughly 24% per year. USO must rely on rapidly rising oil prices just to stay even against these structural headwinds.

Monthly Contango Cost Annualized Roll Drag What It Means
1% per month ~13% per year Oil must rise 13%+ just to break even
2% per month ~24% per year Catastrophic for long-term holders
4.8% (documented example) ~75% annualized April–May 2014 roll, single-month cost

Historical Tracking Error — The Proof

The divergence between oil prices and USO's actual performance has been documented repeatedly across market cycles:

  • 2016: Crude oil rallied 27% for the full year on a continuous chart. USO dramatically underperformed due to persistent contango roll costs throughout the year.
  • 2009: Oil bottomed at $35 and topped $80 — roughly a 128% move on the headline. USO captured a fraction of that gain as contango eroded every monthly roll.
  • 10-Year (to Jan 2022): USO returned approximately -14.6% annualized — during a period when oil itself had meaningful multi-year recoveries.
  • 10-Year Fund Flows (to 2026): Net outflows of approximately $4.68 billion. Institutional money has been leaving consistently — not because oil is unattractive, but because USO is a structurally inefficient vehicle for long-term exposure.
  • 3-Year Flows: Additional $566M in net outflows, confirming the trend is ongoing.

A Real Cautionary Example

In 2016, WTI crude rallied 96.9% from February 12 to year-end — an extraordinary gain. An investor who bought USO at the bottom expecting to capture that move received only 43.6% — less than half the underlying commodity's return. The difference was almost entirely contango and negative roll yield accumulated across each monthly roll. The investor was right about oil. The vehicle still cost them 53 percentage points of potential return.

You can be correct about the direction of oil prices and still lose money — or severely underperform — in USO if contango is steep enough. This is the most dangerous misconception retail investors bring to this ETF.

USO's current surge is partially — perhaps substantially — a crisis fear premium. If the Strait of Hormuz situation de-escalates, crude could retrace sharply and rapidly. A $120 oil price driven by fear of supply disruption is fundamentally different from $120 oil driven by structural demand growth. The premium can vanish faster than it was built.

What History Says About Crisis Oil Spikes

Oil's history is a repeating sequence: geopolitical fear creates violent spikes; resolution or adaptation creates equally violent reversals.

  • 1973 OPEC embargo: Quadrupled oil prices. Triggered global inflation and recession. Then reversed.
  • 1979 Iranian Revolution: Doubled prices within months. Then a full decade of decline followed.
  • 1990 Iraq/Kuwait: Sharp spike on invasion fears. Resolved within months.
  • 2008 Financial Crisis: Oil hit $147. Collapsed to $35 in five months — one of the fastest reversals in commodity history.
  • 2014–16 Shale Glut: 70% decline from peak. One of the three largest oil crashes since WWII.
  • April 2020: WTI futures went negative (-$37/barrel) for the first time in history.
  • February 2022 Ukraine: Brent hit $130. Retraced significantly within months as markets adapted.

The pattern is consistent. USO purchased at the peak of a crisis fear event has historically delivered poor outcomes. The entry point is as important as the thesis.

Section 05
The 2020 Warning

The April 2020 oil crisis is the single most important case study for any USO investor. It exposed structural vulnerabilities that most retail participants did not know existed — and proved that USO can deviate dramatically from its stated purpose under extreme market conditions.

What Happened to Oil

COVID-19 lockdowns destroyed demand globally. Simultaneously, Russia and Saudi Arabia engaged in a production war, flooding markets with oversupply. Storage facilities — particularly at Cushing, Oklahoma — filled to near-capacity. Holders of expiring May 2020 futures faced a stark choice: take physical delivery of 1,000 barrels per contract (impossible for most) or sell at any price. On April 20, 2020, WTI May futures settled at -$37.63 per barrel — the first negative oil price in recorded history.

What Happened to USO

USO was caught in a cascade of structural failures that revealed the fund's deep fragility:

  • In a single week, USCF changed USO's portfolio structure three separate times — shifting from front-month only, to 80/20 split, to 40/55/5 across June/July/August, to "any available contract in any percentage."
  • These structural changes deviated USO fundamentally from its stated tracking objective, leaving holders in a product that no longer did what they thought it did.
  • The fund's mandatory, publicly disclosed roll schedule allowed sophisticated traders to front-run its forced transactions, extracting value at retail shareholders' expense.
  • Without the restructuring, USO could have faced a scenario where its held contracts also went negative — an unprecedented and catastrophic outcome for a retail fund.

Kyle Bass, Hayman Capital CIO, warned publicly during the crisis: "Retail has been plowing into these oil contracts thinking they're buying spot crude oil when they're buying the next front month. Retail investors are going to get fleeced if they continue to fly into these oil ETFs."

The Lesson

The 2020 crisis demonstrated that under extreme market stress, USCF can and will change USO's investment parameters mid-position to protect the fund's survival — regardless of what investors thought they owned. This is not a criticism; it was a necessary response. But it is essential information: USO's structure is not fixed. Under sufficient stress, the product can change.

Section 07
What Moves Oil

Supply-Side Forces

  • OPEC+ production decisions: Saudi Arabia and Russia collude to set global supply baselines. OPEC+ cuts push prices up; production wars crash them (2014–16, 2020).
  • U.S. shale production: America's shale revolution made the U.S. the world's largest producer — a permanent and responsive supply variable that moderates price spikes.
  • Geopolitical disruption: Conflicts affecting major producers (Middle East, Russia, Nigeria, Libya) create sudden supply fear premiums. The current Hormuz crisis is a textbook example.
  • Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR) releases: The U.S. can inject supply to dampen crisis spikes — deployed in 2022 after Ukraine, a policy tool that caps upside moves.
  • Infrastructure and transit chokepoints: The Strait of Hormuz (20% of global supply), Suez Canal, and Bosphorus are permanent leverage points in global oil markets.

Demand-Side Forces

  • Global economic growth: Oil demand correlates tightly with industrial activity, transportation, and manufacturing. Recessions destroy demand (2008, 2020) faster than supply can adjust.
  • China and emerging markets: China is the single largest marginal demand driver for oil globally. Its economic cycle is the most important long-run demand variable outside the U.S.
  • Energy transition: Long-run structural demand reduction as EVs and renewables scale. This is a decade-long process, not an immediate price driver — but it creates a ceiling on long-term oil price expectations.
  • Seasonality: Refineries increase crude purchases before summer driving season. Heating oil demand spikes in winter. Seasonal patterns create predictable demand rhythms.

Macro Financial Forces

  • U.S. Dollar strength: Oil is priced in USD globally. A stronger dollar makes oil more expensive for foreign buyers, suppressing demand and price. A weaker dollar (the current trend) lifts oil prices — this has been a meaningful tailwind in 2025–26.
  • Real interest rates: Higher rates increase storage costs, steepening contango. Lower rates reduce carry costs, flattening the futures curve — favorable for USO's roll economics.
  • Speculative futures positioning: Hedge fund and managed money positions can amplify price moves well beyond what fundamentals justify, creating momentum-driven overshoots in both directions.
  • G7 Strategic Reserve coordination: A coordinated emergency reserve release can inject 60–100M barrels into the market within weeks — a political ceiling on crisis spikes that governments have used repeatedly.

For the former, USO's liquidity makes it hard to beat during crisis conditions. For the latter, equity-based instruments are structurally superior.

Section 09
The Conscious Decision

The following framework cuts through the complexity to the questions that actually determine whether USO is right for you — specifically in the context of March 2026 and the New World Capital structure.

Time Horizon Is Everything

Short Term — Days to Weeks ✓

If you believe the Hormuz crisis will escalate further, USO offers liquid, leveraged exposure to that specific move. In the current steep backwardation, USO's monthly roll actually benefits you. Entry now is buying into crisis momentum. This is the valid use case — but requires a defined exit trigger.

Long Term — Months to Years ✗

USO is structurally inappropriate as a long-term hold. Contango will erode returns once the crisis normalizes. The 10-year outflow of $4.68B reflects this reality. If your thesis is that oil will be higher in 2 years, an energy ETF or oil company stock is a far more efficient vehicle.

Questions to Answer Before Any Position

  1. What is my holding period? Days or weeks = potentially valid given current backwardation. Months or years = structurally wrong vehicle for the reasons documented above.
  2. Can I articulate exactly why the Hormuz crisis will escalate or persist? If you cannot answer this with conviction based on specific intelligence, you are speculating on fear, not investing in oil.
  3. Do I know the current state of the futures curve? Is the market in backwardation (favorable) or beginning to normalize toward contango as the crisis fades?
  4. What is my exit trigger? A defined price target? A de-escalation headline? A time stop? Without a pre-defined exit, you will likely hold through the reversion.
  5. What percentage of the portfolio would this represent? USO swung 14.13% in a single day on March 10. Position sizing must reflect this — not its nominal dollar cost.
  6. Could I express this thesis more efficiently through XLE or an energy stock? If your view is that oil companies will profit from high prices, equity captures that without futures decay.

How USO Fits the New World Capital Framework

New World Capital's core architecture is diversification across distinct structural drivers — each position carrying its own thesis and time horizon. PAXG captures fiat debasement over years. XMR captures the privacy layer over cycles. TAO captures the AI compute thesis over decades. USO is a categorically different instrument — cyclical, geopolitically driven, with no structural long-horizon tailwind.

If USO enters the portfolio, it enters as a tactical trade, not a structural hold. That distinction must be explicit and pre-committed — because the discipline of knowing why you are in and exactly when you will leave is what separates a conscious position from reactive speculation.

USO is a weapon, not a savings vehicle. Used with precision — defined thesis, defined size, defined exit — it captures crisis-driven oil moves with exceptional efficiency. Used without discipline, its structural mechanics and extreme volatility work silently against you. The current environment makes it tempting. That is precisely when the framework matters most.

USO is not what most retail investors assume it is. It does not track the oil price over time. It has no income. It has a structural mechanism — contango roll decay — that erodes returns in normal markets. And it can deviate from its stated structure under extreme stress. The conscious investor enters USO knowing exactly what they own, why they own it, for how long, and what will cause them to exit. Without those four elements defined in advance, USO's volatility and complexity are more likely to work against you than for you.
