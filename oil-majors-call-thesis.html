What Actually Happened

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel conducted joint military strikes on Iran, including the killing of Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. Iran retaliated immediately — launching missile and drone attacks on US military bases, Israeli territory, and Gulf states. The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps issued warnings prohibiting vessel passage through the Strait of Hormuz, and within 48 hours commercial tanker traffic had collapsed by approximately 70%, with over 150 ships anchoring outside the strait to wait out the risk. By March 2, an IRGC commander officially confirmed the strait was closed and threatened to set ablaze any vessel attempting to transit.

Major container shipping companies — Maersk, CMA CGM, and Hapag-Lloyd — suspended all transits. Simultaneously, Houthi-controlled Yemen announced it would resume attacks on commercial ships in the Red Sea, forcing Suez Canal traffic to reroute around Africa's Cape of Good Hope. At least five tankers were struck, two crew members killed, and the Stena Imperative was struck twice at the port of Bahrain. This is not a geopolitical risk premium event. This is real supply destruction in real time.

Why This Does Not Resolve Quickly

Markets are pricing in a quick diplomatic solution. Bob McNally of Rapidan Energy Group noted on March 10 that there is "a lot of optimism in the market" — and that optimism is exactly the mispricing this thesis is built to exploit. The political, operational, and infrastructural realities of the Hormuz crisis make rapid resolution structurally unlikely.

Khamenei is dead. Iran has no singular negotiating counterpart for weeks or months as leadership consolidates. Insurance premiums are at six-year highs, and insurers have stated they will not return until there is 100% certainty of safety — which means even a genuine ceasefire announcement will not reopen shipping immediately. Qatar's LNG plant has never been fully taken offline before; according to Rapidan Energy, it will take weeks, not days, to restart after the all-clear is given. Iraq's southern fields are down 70%, storage is at capacity, and production cannot resume without a functioning export route.

The Saudi East-West Pipeline and the UAE's Fujairah pipeline offer partial alternatives, but terminal infrastructure at Jeddah limits throughput to a fraction of normal Hormuz volumes. The G7 has asked the IEA to prepare scenarios for a coordinated emergency stockpile release — but the combined 1.2 billion barrel reserve buys days or weeks, not months. These are bridges, not solutions.

"Traders assumed for decades that no country would be allowed to shut the Strait. The fact that it has happened at all is completely calamitous and unexpected." — Bob McNally, Rapidan Energy Group

XOM & CVX — The Fundamental Case

ExxonMobil and Chevron are not pure-play crude bets. They are vertically integrated energy majors with refining, chemicals, LNG, and upstream production — meaning they benefit from the oil price spike and the supply chain disruption simultaneously. Their balance sheets were rebuilt post-2020 to withstand low oil environments, which means at $80+ WTI they are generating exceptional free cash flow. And critically: both companies produce primarily from the Permian Basin — they have zero Hormuz exposure.

XOM's Pioneer acquisition added approximately 600,000 barrels per day of low-cost Permian production. CVX's Tengiz expansion and Guyana exposure (via Hess) further insulate it from Gulf supply disruptions. While Asian refiners scramble for Atlantic basin barrels and European gas markets spike, XOM and CVX are positioned as premium non-Gulf suppliers with available capacity. The disruption doesn't threaten them — it benefits them.

Global Impact — Who Bears the Cost

The Hormuz closure is not primarily a US problem — it is an Asian energy crisis. In 2024, 84% of crude oil and condensate shipments through the strait were destined for Asian markets. China, India, Japan, and South Korea accounted for approximately 70% of all flows. Japan and South Korea obtain roughly 95% of their crude from Middle Eastern sources. India faces simultaneous physical and financial shock — 60% of its oil imports and 53% of its LNG come from the Gulf, and much of that LNG is Brent-indexed, meaning a crude spike simultaneously raises LNG contract prices.

China is exposed but more flexible — it holds 7.6 million tons of LNG inventory as of late February and is pivoting further toward Russian crude to offset lost Gulf barrels. But even China cannot absorb the gap without tightening the Pacific basin for all buyers. Europe gets 12–14% of its LNG from Qatar through the strait — and European natural gas prices rose 63% last week alone, the largest weekly gain since Russia's invasion of Ukraine.

None of this affects XOM or CVX production. Permian output is not stranded. US shale is not subject to Gulf shipping routes. The disruption that harms Asia's refiners is the same disruption that creates premium pricing for non-Gulf barrels — exactly what XOM and CVX produce.
