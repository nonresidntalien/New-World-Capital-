Why Stablecoins Alone Are Not a Savings Strategy

If your entire savings is denominated in stablecoins — DAI, USDC, USDT — you are holding dollars. Nothing more. Each of those tokens tracks the USD at a 1:1 ratio, which means that over time, your purchasing power declines at exactly the rate of inflation. In the United States, the cumulative inflation since 2000 has eroded the dollar by more than 70%. A dollar of savings in 2000 buys less than 30 cents of equivalent goods today.

Stablecoins are excellent for liquidity, DeFi yield, and short-term capital preservation. They are not suited for long-term savings because they are architecturally designed to track the purchasing power of a fiat currency that governments can and do inflate at will. Holding only stablecoins is not saving — it is slow-motion loss.

Gold solves this. Over the same period since 2000, gold has risen from approximately $280 per ounce to $5,228 today — a gain of roughly 1,767%. It has not merely kept pace with inflation. It has vastly outrun it. And it has done so through a remarkable string of crises, each of which would have wiped out the purchasing power of savers holding cash or cash equivalents.

📐 The Core Arithmetic
$1,000 saved in USDT in 2000 = ~$1,000 in nominal terms today, but ~$290 in real purchasing power (inflation-adjusted).

$1,000 invested in gold in 2000 (at ~$280/oz) = ~$18,671 today at $5,228/oz — nearly 19× in nominal terms and roughly 5× in real inflation-adjusted terms.

This is why gold is not a speculative asset. It is the benchmark definition of a store of value over multi-decade horizons.

Second, de-dollarization. With global debt at all-time highs and governments in the US, Europe, and Japan structurally dependent on low borrowing costs, the window for sustained high real rates is narrow. Every rate cut cycle historically unleashes the next gold leg up.
